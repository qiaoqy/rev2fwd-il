# Self-Improving Autonomous Robot Learning through Temporal Inversion and Critic-Guided Data Acquisition

## Abstract

Many robot tasks exhibit a fundamental asymmetry: solving a task such as insertion, stacking, or threading is significantly harder than unsolving it such as extraction, toppling, or unthreading. While time-reversing easy-task demonstrations provides a natural source of hard-task training data, naively reversed trajectories alone yield suboptimal policies because learned policies accumulate per-step errors, producing rollout trajectories that progressively diverge from the demonstrations and are unsuitable as self-training signal. We observe that this very divergence, when viewed in reverse, naturally introduces diverse initial conditions that enrich the training distribution. Building on this insight, we propose an iterative framework that rolls out an easy-task policy on the easy task, temporally inverts the collected trajectories as candidate hard-task data, and uses a learned critic network trained on the hard policy's own rollouts with sparse terminal rewards to estimate per-frame advantage values and selectively retain only high-quality segments. The refined data, combined with the original demonstrations, updates the hard-task policy, while the easy and hard policies reset the environment for each other, forming a closed loop that eliminates incremental human demonstrations and manual interaction beyond the initial data collection. Across multiple simulated tasks, our method consistently improves hard-task success rates to near-optimal levels within a small number of iterations, matching or exceeding policies trained on expert hard-task demonstrations, while achieving superior data efficiency and training stability compared to reinforcement learning alternatives.
